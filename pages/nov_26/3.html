The ceiling fan was the only thing moving in Elias’s world, and even that was struggling. It clicked rhythmically—click, whir, click, whir—a metronome counting down the seconds of a life that felt like it had already ended. Elias lay on a mattress that smelled of stale sweat and old sickness. His body was a map of failures. The chemotherapy had hollowed him out, leaving his skin translucent and his bones sharp enough to cut. But the cancer was just the physical tenant; the landlord was the darkness in his head. The anxiety was a constant hum, a swarm of bees behind his eyes. You are a burden. You are expiring. There is no point. The depression was the gravity that pinned him to the sheets, making the act of reaching for a glass of water feel like climbing Everest. He looked at the nightstand. Three orange pill bottles. A glass of water. And a letter from the bank about foreclosure he hadn't opened in weeks. "Today," Elias whispered to the cracking plaster of the ceiling. "I think today is the day I stop." It wasn't a dramatic declaration. It was just a surrender. He closed his eyes, welcoming the void. He let the panic rise, let the grief of his wasted years wash over him, preparing to let go of the tether. Then, the world went black. A power cut. The fan died. The streetlights outside blinked out. The sudden silence was deafening. In the absolute darkness, Elias’s anxiety spiked. The bees swarmed louder. He couldn't see his hands. He felt like he was already in the grave. His breath came in short, ragged gasps. This is it, he thought. The end is just darkness. Knock. Knock. Knock. The sound was small, hesitant. Elias froze. He wanted to ignore it. He wanted to die in peace. Knock. Knock. "Mr. Elias?" A child’s voice. Tiny. Trembling. Elias groaned, the sound scraping his throat. He swung his legs off the bed, his joints popping, pain shooting through his spine. He used the wall to navigate, his fingers tracing the peeling paint until he found the door handle. He opened it. Standing in the hallway was Maya, the seven-year-old girl from the apartment across the hall. Her mother worked double shifts; Maya was often alone in the evenings. She was holding a single, sputtering candle in a jar. Her eyes were wide, filled with a terror that Elias recognized. It was the same terror he lived with every day. The fear of the dark. He saw his own fear reflected in her face. But in that mirror, he saw something else. He saw a child who needed him. For the first time in months, the bees in his head went silent. The candle flickered, casting long, dancing shadows against the wall. "I'm scared," Maya admitted, hugging her knees. "Me too," Elias said. It was the most honest thing he had said in years. Maya looked at him, surprised. "You? But you're a grown-up." "Grown-ups get scared too, Maya. Sometimes the dark feels very big."
