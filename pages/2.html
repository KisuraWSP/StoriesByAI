The video was forty-two seconds long. That was all it took to dismantle forty-five years of quiet, invisible existence. Sugath was a man of beige. He wore beige trousers, drove a beige sedan, and lived a life of beige neutrality. He was an accountant who double-checked his receipts and never parked in the "No Parking" zone. He was not a villain. He was just tired. But the internet does not care about fatigue. It does not care about the heatstroke inducing humidity of a Tuesday afternoon, or the fact that Sugath’s diabetic mother had just been rushed to the ICU, or that the delivery rider had cut across three lanes of traffic and smashed the side mirror off Sugath’s car. It captured the ugliest three words Sugath had ever spoken in a moment of blind adrenaline. It didn't capture the apology he offered two minutes later. It didn't capture him handing the boy a thousand rupees for the trouble. It only captured the rage. By Wednesday morning, Sugath was trending.
